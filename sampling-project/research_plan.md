## 2. Research Questions

### RQ1 [Topline — Error prevalence by cohort]

**What are the most frequent grammatical error types in M2 and M3 learner writing, and how do their relative frequency rankings differ between the two cohorts?**

*Approach:* Aggregate `errant_analysis.errors[*].count` by error type, normalised per 100 words, separately for M2 and M3. Produce ranked tables and side-by-side comparison of the top-15 error types per cohort. Prior research on proficiency-level error profiling (Beginners: tense/verb-form dominant; Advanced: determiners, prepositions, complex structures) provides an expected gradient against which to compare findings.

*Variables:* Cohort (M2/M3, categorical IV); error type frequency (continuous DV, normalised per 100 words).

---

### RQ2 [Topline — Error-rate thresholding]

**To what extent do M2 and M3 cohorts differ in the proportion of students whose mean error rate exceeds a threshold of (a) 10%, (b) 15%, (c) 20%, and (d) 25%?**

*Approach:* Compute per-student aggregate error rate (mean across all their submissions). Evaluate cumulative distribution at each threshold, split by cohort. Benchmark candidate thresholds against the Write & Improve Corpus 2024 (Nicholls et al.), which provides CEFR-labelled error-rate norms. Produce histograms of error rates by cohort with threshold lines overlaid.

*Variables:* Cohort (M2/M3); error-rate threshold (ordinal, 4 levels); binary classification at each threshold.

---

### RQ3 [Topline — Between-cohort error composition]

**To what extent does the distribution of grammatical error types differ between active (M2) and former (M3) students when controlling for text length, and which specific error categories exhibit the largest divergence between the two groups?**

*Approach:* Normalise per-student error counts by word count (errors per 100 words). Apply Mann-Whitney U or permutation tests per error type with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. Report effect sizes (Cliff's δ) and rank error types by magnitude of difference. Generate a differential heatmap. The comparison is between students who stayed in the program (M2 label) and those who left (M3 label). A systematic difference in error profiles — particularly if former students show higher rates of specific error types — may signal difficulties that contributed to attrition.

*Variables:* Enrollment status (active=M2, former=M3, IV); normalised error count per type (DV per test); word count (covariate).

*Preliminary finding:* Mean error rate 14.97% (active) vs 16.44% (former). Small overall difference, but distribution shapes differ markedly at the upper end (66.7% of M3 vs 38.9% of M2 exceed 15% threshold).

---

### RQ4 [Within-student — Error profile consistency across topics]

**How consistent is an individual student's error rate and error-type profile across multiple writing samples, and to what extent is within-student error variability attributable to topic versus unexplained individual variation?**

*Note on longitudinal analysis:* The original temporal framing of this question was invalidated because `submission_date` records batch-upload timestamps, not actual submission dates. Within-year temporal trajectories cannot be reliably estimated. The multi-submission data is retained for within-student *cross-topic* consistency analysis.

*Approach:*
1. Variance decomposition via ICC(1): Fit a null random-intercepts model (`error_rate ~ (1 | student_id)`) and compute the intraclass correlation coefficient. ICC close to 1 means error rate is stable within students (a single sample is diagnostic); ICC close to 0 means high within-student variability (multiple samples needed).
2. Topic-driven variance: Add topic category as a fixed effect (`error_rate ~ topic_category + (1 | student_id)`) to measure the proportion of within-student variance explained by topic.
3. Per-type consistency: For the top-5 error types, compute Kendall's W (coefficient of concordance) across each student's submissions. Assess whether a student's error-type *ranking* is stable or topic-dependent.

*Variables:* Student ID (random intercept); topic category (IV); error rate; per-type error counts (DVs).

---

### RQ5 [Methodology — Overcorrection and model reliability]

**To what extent is the automated overcorrection rate associated with higher overall error rates, larger edit spans, or enrollment status, and does this relationship suggest the correction model is less reliable for certain students?**

*Approach:*
1. **Group comparison:** Mann-Whitney U comparing mean `overcorrection_ratio` between active (M2) and former (M3) groups. If former students have higher ratios, model reliability may be worse for students who later left.
2. **Correlational:** Spearman's ρ between `overcorrection_ratio` and (a) `error_rate`, (b) `multi_token_edits`. If positive, the correction model is least reliable for high-error students and multi-token edits may signal overreach.
3. **Edit span analysis:** Spearman's ρ between `multi_token_edits` and `overcorrection_count`. Wide edits combined with high overcorrection suggest wholesale rewriting rather than minimal-edit correction — a known failure mode in LLM-based GEC.

*Variables:* Overcorrection ratio (DV); enrollment status (IV); error rate, edit span (predictors).

---

### RQ6 [Cohort — Topic sensitivity]

**To what extent does the writing task topic influence the frequency and distribution of grammatical error types in M2 and M3 EFL learner writing?**

*Approach:* Group submissions by the `topic` field after standardisation (see V8). Compute per-topic error-type profiles, normalised per 100 words. Test the hypothesis that narrative prompts elicit more R:VERB:TENSE and R:PUNCT errors, while argumentative/transactional prompts increase R:WO, R:CONJ, and M:DET errors. Kruskal-Wallis test per error type across topic categories (post-hoc Dunn's with Bonferroni). χ² confound check: test whether topic distribution differs between M2 and M3 (if significant, RQ3 results may be partially confounded by topic).

*Variables:* Topic (categorical IV); normalised error-type counts (DVs); cohort (moderator).

---

### RQ7 [Cohort — Sub-class effects]

**To what extent do mean error rates and error-type profiles differ between the two academic sub-classes within the active-student cohort (M3-4A vs M3-5A, both labelled M2 in the data), and can such differences be attributed to instructional effects rather than intake variation?**

*Approach:* Cross-reference `student_id` with the `classlists` Supabase table to assign sub-class (M3-4A or M3-5A). Only the 36 active students have classlist entries; former (M3-labelled) students have unknown sub-class and are excluded. Mann-Whitney U comparing per-student mean error rate and per-type error counts between the two sub-classes (Cliff's δ effect sizes). Confound check: χ² test for topic distribution across sub-classes.

*Power warning:* n=18 per sub-class. Differences need to be large (Cliff's δ > 0.5) to reach significance at α=0.05. Report effect sizes regardless of p-values.

---

### RQ8 [Cohort — Text length and error rate]

**What is the nature and magnitude of the relationship between text length (word count) and grammatical error rate in M2 and M3 learner writing, and to what extent does this relationship differ between cohorts?**

*Approach:* Spearman's ρ between `word_count` and `error_rate` per cohort and combined. Fisher's z-transformation to test moderation by cohort. LOESS-smoothed scatterplots.

---

### RQ9 [Methodology — Uncategorised errors]

**What proportion of edits identified by the ERRANT annotation pipeline remain uncategorised, and what linguistic features characterise the errors that the `post_classify_other()` fallback fails to resolve?**

*Approach:* Aggregate `errant_analysis.uncategorised` across all files. Compute uncategorised ratio per file. Sample 20 uncategorised edit pairs from the full range. Manually label linguistic phenomena: L1-transfer, idiom, discourse reformulation, model hallucination, genuine ERRANT taxonomy gap.

---

### RQ10 [Individual — Error-profile clustering]

**To what extent can M2 and M3 EFL learners be grouped into distinct clusters on the basis of their normalised error-type profiles, and what are the defining error-type characteristics of each cluster?**

*Approach:* PCA (≥80% variance explained) + k-means (k=2–6, elbow + silhouette selection) + hierarchical clustering for consensus on per-student normalised error vectors. t-SNE visualisation. Bootstrap cluster stability via Adjusted Rand Index. Interpret clusters qualitatively (e.g., "determiner-dominant," "verb-form-dominant," "uniformly low-error").

---

### RQ11 [Individual — Outlier detection]

**Which individual learners in each cohort demonstrate outlier error patterns — in terms of exceptionally high error rate, single-type error dominance, or high overcorrection rate — and what do these profiles suggest about the appropriate use of automated error analysis for such students?**

*Approach:* Per student: (a) mean error rate, (b) dominance ratio (most frequent type count / total errors), (c) overcorrection ratio. Flag students exceeding 2σ above cohort mean on any metric. Cross-reference flagged students against `summary` feedback.

---

### RQ12 [Individual — Feedback accuracy]

**To what extent does the AI-generated summary feedback align with the quantitative error-type distribution for each student, and which error types are systematically under-represented or over-represented in the qualitative feedback text?**

*Approach:* Stratified sample of 40 files (20 per cohort, by error rate quintile). Regex extraction of error types mentioned in `summary` text vs actual top-5 from `errant_analysis.errors`. Precision, recall, F1 per student. Per-type over/under-representation rates. Wilcoxon signed-rank test for precision vs recall asymmetry across the sample.

---

### RQ13 [Methodology — Majority-voting reliability]

**To what extent does the dual-temperature majority-voting mechanism produce divergent edit annotations between passes, and are certain grammatical error types disproportionately affected by inter-pass annotation disagreement?**

*Approach:* Compute `uncertainty_ratio = uncertain_edit_count / total_edit_count` per file. Descriptive statistics per cohort. Spearman correlation with `error_rate`. If edit-level metadata can be persisted from `intersect_edits()`, compute per-type disagreement rates.

---

### RQ14 [Methodology — Edit span and error type]

**What is the relationship between edit span (number of tokens affected by a correction) and error type classification, and do multi-token edits correlate with elevated overcorrection rates?**

*Approach:* Spearman correlations between `multi_token_edits` / `avg_span` and `overcorrection_count`. If per-type edit span data can be logged, test whether morphological errors (R:SPELL, R:MORPH) produce narrower edits than syntactic errors (R:WO, R:VERB:TENSE).

## 3. Statistical Analysis Plan

All analyses use Python with `scipy`, `statsmodels`, `scikit-learn`, and `pandas`. Significance threshold α = 0.05. Bonferroni correction applied to all multi-test families (adjusted α = 0.05 / k).

| RQ | Test(s) | Correction | Effect size |
|----|---------|------------|-------------|
| RQ1 | Descriptive (ranked frequencies) | — | — |
| RQ2 | Descriptive (CDF, threshold proportions) | — | — |
| RQ3 | Mann-Whitney U per error type | Bonferroni across k types | Cliff's δ |
| RQ4 | ICC via mixed-effects model; Kendall's W | — | ICC, W |
| RQ5 | Mann-Whitney U; Spearman's ρ | — | ρ |
| RQ6 | Kruskal-Wallis; Dunn's post-hoc | Bonferroni | ε² |
| RQ7 | Mann-Whitney U | Bonferroni | Cliff's δ |
| RQ8 | Spearman's ρ; Fisher's z | — | ρ with 95% CI |
| RQ9 | Descriptive ratios; Spearman correlates | — | ρ |
| RQ10 | PCA + k-means (k=2–6) + hierarchical; bootstrap | Silhouette | ARI |
| RQ11 | Threshold-based (2σ per cohort) | — | — |
| RQ12 | Precision/Recall/F1; Wilcoxon signed-rank | — | F1 |
| RQ13 | Spearman's ρ | — | ρ |
| RQ14 | Spearman's ρ; Kruskal-Wallis | — | ρ, ε² |

### Rationale for non-parametric choices

Error-type counts are zero-inflated and right-skewed. Most students have zero for most error types. Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis, and Spearman's ρ do not assume normality and are robust to outliers and zero-inflation. Cliff's δ is the corresponding non-parametric effect size for Mann-Whitney.

## 4. Data Validation Plan (8+1 Checks)

Validation runs as a pre-analysis gate. No analysis proceeds on unvalidated data.

### V1. File-level completeness
- **Check:** Required fields (`student_id`, `class`, `error_rate`, `word_count`, `errant_analysis`, `metadata`) present per file
- **Threshold:** >5% missing → field excluded
- **Output:** Completeness report — field × % present

### V2. Structural integrity
- `errant_analysis.errors` is a list with `{type, count, example}` entries
- `metadata` sub-fields are non-negative integers
- **Action:** Flag corrupt files; exclude from affected analysis

### V3. Value range
- `error_rate`: 0–100
- `word_count`: ≥1
- `overcorrection_count` ≤ `total_edit_count`
- **Action:** Obvious pipeline errors excluded; edge cases retained but noted

### V4. Cohort assignment verification
- Cross-reference every `student_id` against Supabase `classlists` table
- Verify consistency: "M2" files should have student_id in classlists; "M3" files should NOT
- **Action:** Mismatches logged; source of truth is `classlists` table

### V5. Duplicate detection
- Same `student_id` + same `word_count` + same `error_rate` → possible re-run artifact
- **Action:** Keep first/ most complete; log exclusion

### V6. Submission date consistency
- Verify ISO 8601 format. Flag dates outside academic year range
- **Note:** `submission_date` is a batch-upload timestamp, not actual writing date. Temporal analysis is limited to academic year level

### V7. Error count vs total_edit_count consistency
- Compute: `delta = total_edit_count - Σ errors[i].count - len(uncategorised)`
- Expected: small delta (UNK/U:SPACE edits that `classify_edits()` silently drops)
- **Threshold:** |delta| ≥ 5 flagged
- **Audit finding (current data):** 481 files have delta=0, 199 have delta -1 to -4, **5 files have delta 81–332** (these need investigation before error-type-level analysis)

### V8. Topic field standardisation
- Merge near-duplicate topic values (e.g., "My favorite Movie/TV series" × 4 variants)
- **Action:** Manual canonicalisation. Files with missing topic excluded from RQ6 only

### V9. Historical_data deduplication
- `local-working/historical_data.json`: 19 entries, 10 unique. Deduplicate to 10 entries
- **Note:** Secondary data source; does not affect primary research files

## 6. Data Quality Caveats

1. **Class-label convention:** M2 = currently enrolled (in classlists), M3 = left the program (not in classlists). This is NOT academic year level. Consistent across all 689 files (zero anomalies).

2. **Sentence pair alignment is broken:** The `align_sentences()` in `errant_analysis.py` uses greedy one-to-one matching that misaligns when source and target have different sentence counts. The `sentence_pairs` field is unreliable. This does NOT affect error-type counts, metadata, or summary feedback — only the sentence-alignment field.

3. **Submission dates are batch timestamps:** `submission_date` records when data was uploaded, not when the student wrote the essay. Within-year temporal analysis (pre/post comparisons, time trends) is not valid. The `academic_year` field in `writing_assessment_cambridge` provides the only reliable temporal dimension.

4. **5 high-delta files:** 5 files have `total_edit_count` far exceeding the sum of classified errors (delta 81–332). These likely have large numbers of silently dropped UNK/U:SPACE edits. They should be excluded from error-type-level analyses until investigated.

5. **9 duplicate entries in historical_data.json:** 19 rows → 10 unique. Does not affect primary research files.

6. **Majority-voting metadata granularity:** `uncertain_edit_count` is stored at file level, not per error type. Error-type-level disagreement analysis (deeper RQ13) requires pipeline modification to persist per-type disagreement.

7. **Sub-class scope:** RQ7 is limited to the 36 active students (M3-4A vs M3-5A). The 36 former students have no sub-class assignment and cannot be included.
